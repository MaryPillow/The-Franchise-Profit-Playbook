Why Labor % Is Lying to You

Mary Pillow Thompson
Franchise Consultant · Author
← Back to Blog

Labor percentage is the most commonly watched number in franchise operations. It appears on every P&L review, every franchisor report, and every end-of-week debrief. And it is leading a lot of operators in exactly the wrong direction.

Not because the math is wrong. Because the metric is lagging — and lagging metrics tell you what already happened, not what you can do about it today.

"By the time your labor % shows up on a report, the shift is over. The schedule is set. The money is already spent."

The problem with percentage-based thinking

Labor % is a ratio: labor dollars divided by sales. That means two very different things can produce the same number — and treating them the same way will get you in trouble.

A 32% labor week where sales were unexpectedly low looks identical on paper to a 32% labor week where you were overstaffed. But the right response to each scenario is completely different. One calls for a sales push. The other calls for a scheduling adjustment. If you are managing to the percentage alone, you cannot tell them apart.

What labor % can't tell you

  • Whether you were overstaffed or under-sold
  • Which shifts drove the variance — and why
  • Whether cutting hours would have cost you revenue
  • What the right staffing level actually is for your volume
  • Whether your schedule matches your actual demand pattern

The metric that actually moves behavior

Sales Per Labor Hour — SPLH — is a fundamentally different kind of number. Instead of telling you how much you spent relative to sales, it tells you how productively each labor hour was deployed. And it does this in real time, during the shift, when you can still act on it.

When a manager knows that their shift is running at $48 SPLH against a $55 target, they have a decision to make right now: accelerate sales, adjust floor coverage, or both. Labor % cannot give them that. It can only tell them, three days later, that something went wrong.

"The operators who run tight labor are not cutting hours blindly. They are matching hours to revenue — and they are doing it in real time."

— Mary Pillow Thompson, The Franchise Profit Playbook

What to do with this

You do not have to abandon labor % entirely. Use it as a historical benchmark. But run your operations on a forward-looking number — SPLH target by daypart, tracked in real time during the shift.

  • Calculate your target SPLH for each daypart based on historical volume
  • Post the live SPLH on whatever display your manager has access to
  • Give managers the authority to make staffing micro-adjustments without calling you
  • Review SPLH by shift in your weekly debrief — not just end-of-week labor %

The shift from lagging to leading indicators is not complicated. But it changes everything about how your team makes decisions on the floor.
