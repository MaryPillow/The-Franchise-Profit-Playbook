Why Your $85 SPLH Target Is Mathematically Impossible — And What to Use Instead

Mary Pillow Thompson
Franchise Consultant · Author
← Back to Blog

A lot of franchise operators have a SPLH target. They set it once — usually based on a conversation with their franchisor, a benchmark from another operator, or a rough calculation from their own P&L — and then they use it as the standard for every shift, every daypart, every day of the week.

The problem is that a single flat SPLH target is almost always wrong. Sometimes it is too easy to hit. Sometimes it is genuinely impossible. And when a target is either too easy or too hard, it stops functioning as a target — it becomes noise.

"A SPLH target that doesn't account for daypart and day-of-week isn't an operational tool. It's a number on a spreadsheet."

Why the math doesn't work

Consider what SPLH actually measures: sales dollars generated per labor hour deployed. The numerator (sales) varies enormously by daypart. Your Saturday lunch rush might generate 4x the sales volume of your Tuesday morning opening. If you staff both shifts similarly, the SPLH numbers will look completely different — not because of operational quality, but because of demand variance.

A flat $85 SPLH target during a low-volume Tuesday morning opening might require you to run with a skeleton crew that produces poor customer experience and burns out your team. That same $85 target on a Saturday lunch shift might be trivially easy to exceed with normal staffing. Neither situation gives your manager a useful number to manage against.

The problem with a single SPLH target

  • Demand varies by daypart — your sales potential is not the same at 8am and noon
  • Staffing minimums exist regardless of sales volume — you cannot run with zero labor
  • A target that is too easy stops driving behavior; one that is too hard creates learned helplessness
  • One number cannot reflect the different operational realities of different shifts
  • Managers who manage to the wrong target make worse decisions than managers with no target at all

What to use instead

Build a daypart-specific SPLH matrix. For each daypart — morning, mid, lunch, afternoon, dinner, close — calculate what a realistic SPLH target looks like based on your actual historical volume at that daypart.

This gives your managers a target that is achievable and meaningful for the shift they are actually running. It also gives you a much more accurate diagnostic tool: when a manager misses their daypart-specific SPLH, you know exactly which part of the operation underperformed — not just that the day was rough overall.

"The right SPLH target is the one that requires your manager to make smart decisions to hit it — not so easy they coast, not so hard they give up."

— Mary Pillow Thompson, The Franchise Profit Playbook

How to build your matrix

  • Pull 90 days of shift-level data: sales by hour and labor hours by hour
  • Calculate actual SPLH for each daypart across those 90 days
  • Identify your best-performing and worst-performing instances — this gives you the range
  • Set the target at the 65th–75th percentile for each daypart: achievable on a good shift, but not automatic
  • Review and adjust quarterly as your volume profile changes

The right metric is not better data — it is the right data for the decision being made. A daypart-specific SPLH target gives your managers a real game to play, not a number to report after the fact.
